Understanding your garden

Before you buy a pot, a bag of soil, or a single plant, spend some time with your terrace as it is.
A terrace is not a neutral space. It has an orientation, a microclimate, a load limit, and a drainage system — all of which will shape what you can grow and where. Two terraces in the same building can behave very differently depending on which way they face, what surrounds them, and how exposed they are to wind. You're looking at four things: sun, wind, drainage, and weight. None of this requires specialist knowledge — mostly observation, a clear day, and a walk around after rain. Get this part right and everything that follows becomes easier.

Containers and soils

The ground gardener takes soil for granted. You can't.
In a pot, your plant's entire world is a few litres of growing medium that you chose, placed, and are responsible for. Get the container wrong and drainage fails. Get the soil wrong and roots suffocate or starve. Get both right and you've solved most of the problems beginners run into. This chapter covers what to put your plants in and what to put around their roots — the two decisions that underpin everything else.

What to grow

The temptation is to start with what you want to eat or what looks beautiful.
That's not wrong — but it needs to be filtered through what your terrace can actually support. A plant in the wrong spot doesn't just underperform. It struggles visibly, attracts pests, and discourages you. Match the plant to the conditions first, and the results will follow.

Watering

More potted plants die from overwatering than from drought.
That's worth keeping in mind before you reach for the can. In the ground, excess water disperses. In a pot, it sits. Learning to water well is less about frequency and more about reading your plants and your soil — and resisting the urge to water on a fixed schedule regardless of what you're seeing.

Feeding and fertilizing

Potting mix doesn't last forever..
It starts out with enough nutrients to get a plant established, but a container plant growing through an active season will deplete those reserves faster than you might expect. Feeding is not complicated, but timing and quantity matter. Too little and growth stalls. Too much and you damage the roots you're trying to support.
