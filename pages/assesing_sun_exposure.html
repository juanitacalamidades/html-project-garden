Assesing sun exposure

This is one of the most important things to get right before buying a single plant. Most beginner mistakes — plants that won't flower, vegetables that barely grow, herbs that rot — trace back to a mismatch between what the plant needs and how much sun the spot actually gets.

What the sun labels on plant tags actually mean

Label Hours of direct sun per day
Full sun 6 or more hours
Partial sun / partial shade 3-6 hours
Full shade fewer than 3 hours

"Direct" means the sun is physically hitting the plant, not just the sky being bright. A north-facing terrace can feel light and pleasant to sit in while being useless for tomatoes.

How to assess your terrace — the practical method

You only need to follow these steps once, ideally on a clear day in the season you plan to garden.

  1. Pick your reference day. Do this in spring or early summer. Winter sun is much lower in the sky and will underestimate summer exposure significantly.

  2. Check every 2 hours. At 8am, 10am, 12pm, 2pm, 4pm, and 6pm, go out and note which parts of your terrace are in direct sun and which are in shadow. A rough sketch on paper works perfectly.

  3. Count the hours per zone. Different corners of the same terrace can have very different conditions. A south-facing wall reflects heat and adds hours; a tall neighbouring building can block sun from 2pm onwards.

  4. Note the quality, not just the quantity. Morning sun (east-facing) is gentler. Afternoon sun (west-facing) is hotter and more intense — great for heat-lovers like peppers and tomatoes, but it can scorch leafy greens in midsummer.

Common terrace situations

  • South-facing terrace

    Usually the most productive. Strong sun most of the day. Good for tomatoes, peppers, herbs, and most fruiting plants. Watch for overheating in July–August.

  • North-facing terrace

    The hardest situation. Stick to shade-tolerant plants: ferns, hostas, mint, some lettuces. Don't try to grow tomatoes here — it won't work.

  • East-facing terrace

    Morning sun, afternoon shade. Good for leafy greens, strawberries, and many herbs. Often underestimated — it's actually a pleasant growing environment.

  • West-facing terrace

    Afternoon and evening sun. Hot in summer. Works well for Mediterranean herbs (rosemary, thyme, lavender) and tomatoes.

One thing beginners consistently get wrong

They assess sun in the wrong season. A terrace that gets 7 hours of sun in December may only get 4 in July if a neighbouring building or overhang blocks the higher summer sun — or the reverse. If you can only assess once, do it in May or June, which is closest to peak growing conditions in most of Spain and northern Europe.
